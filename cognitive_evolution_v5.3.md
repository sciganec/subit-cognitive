# Cognitive Evolution of Man: A Structural Model of the Historical Development of Knowledge Externalization

*Version 5.3 · Working Academic Edition · July 12, 2026*

---

## Abstract

This paper proposes a structural model of the historical development of human cognition that explains the cumulative acceleration of cultural change following the emergence of *Homo sapiens*. The central mechanism is not further biological evolution, but the successive externalization of cognitive functions: from corporeal integration with the world through symbolic representation and semiotic fixation to informational autonomization. A formal state space is proposed, constituted by four invariant vectors (I, Thou, They, We) and four historical paradigms (Proto, Sapiens, Ludens, Digitalis), yielding 16 modes. Each mode is identified through five criteria (medium, representation, transmission, scale, autonomy). Version 5.3 establishes the definitive matrix with refined *They* and *We* vectors, along with the modes *Self-Consciousness* (replacing *Reason*), *Information*, and *Data* in Digitalis, strengthening the focus on reflexivity and formal knowledge processing.

**Keywords:** cognitive evolution, externalization of cognition, extended mind, distributed cognition, cultural evolution, historical epistemology, cognitive archaeology.

---

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1. The Problem

Biological evolution explains the origin of *Homo sapiens*, but it does not explain why, after the completion of major morphological changes (approximately 300–200 thousand years ago), the rate of cultural, technological, and social change began to increase exponentially. Anatomically modern humans of the late Paleolithic and contemporary humans are virtually indistinguishable, yet their cognitive capacities, volume of knowledge, and forms of collective activity differ radically. This discrepancy constitutes the central problem that any theory of human development must address.

### 1.2. Central Thesis

The primary cumulative mechanism of post-biological development of human cognitive systems is not further morphological complication of the organism, but cultural-cognitive externalization — the transfer of cognitive functions from the biological carrier to external representational systems, enabling the overcoming of limitations of individual memory, local experience, and spatiotemporal embeddedness.

### 1.3. Research Question

Does the historical process of externalization of cognitive functions possess an invariant morphological structure, and how can this structure be operationalized to identify transitions between cognitive epochs?

---

## 2. Theoretical Foundations and Literature Review

### 2.1. Biological and Cultural Evolution

The Darwinian paradigm explains genetic adaptation, yet the rate of cultural change is several orders of magnitude higher than that of genetic change (Boyd & Richerson, 1985). Henrich (2016) demonstrates that human success is determined by the capacity for cumulative cultural transmission, not individual intelligence. However, theories of cultural evolution address the *how* (mechanisms of transmission) but do not describe *what* qualitatively changes in the structure of cognition.

### 2.2. Cognitive Revolutions and Extended Cognition

Merlin Donald (1991) proposes four stages: episodic, mimetic, mythic, and theoretic, introducing the concept of *external symbolic storage*. Michael Mithen (1996) describes *cognitive fluidity* — the integration of domains in the late Paleolithic. Michael Tomasello (2014) emphasizes *shared intentionality*. Clark & Chalmers (1998) and Hutchins (1995) formulate the theses of the *extended* and *distributed* mind.

These approaches identify individual mechanisms and transitions but do not offer a unified morphology that combines all vectors of cognition (subjective, objective, collective, world-relational) and allows comparison of different historical states. Our model fills this gap by proposing a formal space of cognitive states.

### 2.3. Additional Sources

For empirical grounding of the modes, the following are drawn upon: Tylor (animism, 1871), Service (band-tribe-chiefdom-state, 1962), Maine (status-to-contract, 1861), Jaspers (Axial Age, 1949), Huizinga (play as culture-generator, 1938), Shannon (formalization of information, 1948), Schmandt-Besserat (origin of writing from tokens), d'Huy and Witzel (phylogenetic reconstruction of myth).

## 3. Reconstruction Methodology: Criteria for Identifying a Cognitive Mode

To move from historical reconstruction to an operationalized model, each mode is defined through five criteria. This allows avoidance of arbitrary interpretations.

**Table 1. Criteria for Mode Identification**

| Criterion | Question | Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **Medium** | Where is knowledge stored? | biological memory, artifact, text, database, network |
| **Representation** | How is knowledge encoded? | sensorimotor, symbolic, written, digital |
| **Transmission** | How is knowledge transmitted across time and space? | imitation, oral tradition, written fixation, electronic copy |
| **Scale** | What is the size of the cognitive community? | few individuals → tribe → state → global network |
| **Autonomy** | How independent is knowledge from its carrier? | inseparable, partially separated, highly autonomous |

Each mode receives a profile across these criteria. A transition occurs when at least three of the five criteria change, enabling formal dating of paradigm shifts.

## 4. The Space of Cognitive States: A Formal Model

A cognitive state is defined by four vectors:

**C = (I, Thou, They, We)**

where:
- **I** — the type of subject (carrier of cognition);
- **Thou** — the type of external medium (object);
- **They** — the type of world model;
- **We** — the type of collective coordination.

Each vector takes one of four values corresponding to the historical paradigms. This yields a discrete space of 4⁴ = 256 theoretical states, of which only 16 are realized in actual history, indicating strong constraints on compatibility.

### The Mode Matrix (Central Result)

| Paradigm | **I** (Subject) | **Thou** (Medium) | **They** (World) | **We** (Collective) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Proto** | 1. Consciousness | 2. Body | 3. Nature | 4. Kin |
| **Sapiens** | 5. Self-Consciousness | 6. Tool | 7. Myth | 8. Tribe |
| **Ludens** | 9. Symbol | 10. Text | 11. Ideology | 12. Polis |
| **Digitalis** | 13. Information | 14. Technology | 15. Data | 16. Network |

*Note:* The term "paradigm" is used in a non-Kuhnian sense: a new paradigm does not replace the previous one but expands the space of available cognitive operations; older modes persist as substrates.

## 5. Description of Paradigms and Empirical Modes

### 5.1. Proto — Prehistoric Cognitive Platform

Proto is not a "human paradigm" in the narrow sense, but a basal cognitive platform shared by many species, preceding the symbolic revolution and persisting as a substrate in all subsequent epochs.

- **1. Consciousness.** Pre-symbolic sensorimotor cognition; upper boundary — the emergence of *Homo sapiens* (~300 kya, Jebel Irhoud). Criteria: medium — brain/body; representation — immediate experience; transmission — imitation; scale — individual/small group; autonomy — zero.
- **2. Body.** Mirror self-recognition test (Gallup, 1970) as a marker of self-awareness; phylogenetically broader than *Homo*. Medium — bodily schemas.
- **3. Nature.** Animism (Tylor, 1871); phylogenetic reconstruction (p=0.99); Göbekli Tepe (~9600 BCE) as a ritual center.
- **4. Kin.** Blood-related group. Archaeological anchors: Hazleton North (patrilineal), Fujia (matrilineal), demonstrating variability of a single principle.

---

### 5.2. Sapiens — Symbolic Representation

- **5. Self-Consciousness.** Unlike mere consciousness (awareness of environment), self-consciousness is the capacity to posit one's own "I" as an object of reflection. Emergence of symbolic thinking: ochre engravings at Blombos (~75 kya), burial rites, bodily ornamentation. Criteria: medium — collective memory; representation — oral symbol; transmission — oral tradition; scale — tribe; autonomy — low.
- **6. Tool.** Neolithic Revolution (independently in 6 regions). Technological knowledge extends beyond individual lifespan.
- **7. Myth.** Oral mythic culture (Donald). Phylogenetic reconstruction of the "Cosmic Hunt" myth (d'Huy, ~15–20 kya) — the first global model of the world.
- **8. Tribe.** Ritual-territorial community. Göbekli Tepe as a meeting place for different groups — the transition from kin to tribe.

---

### 5.3. Ludens — Semiotic Fixation

- **9. Symbol.** Writing (4 independent inventions: Mesopotamia ~3400–3100, Egypt ~3250, China by ~1250, Mesoamerica by ~650–500 BCE). Per Schmandt-Besserat, from token accounting systems. Criteria: medium — clay/papyrus; representation — symbolic code; transmission — written fixation; scale — state; autonomy — high.
- **10. Text.** Systematic recording of knowledge: Mesopotamian omen literature ("Enuma Anu Enlil", ~2000–1600 BCE) — text as an instrument of prediction.
- **11. Ideology.** Axial Age (Jaspers, ~800–200 BCE) — reflexive, universalist systems in Greece, Israel, India, China, Persia. The emergence of universal ethical and cosmological schemes beyond local myth.
- **12. Polis.** Greek classical period (~800–400 BCE) — public competitive discourse as a generative element (Huizinga, 1938). The transition from myth to logos, from the sacred to the political.

---

### 5.4. Digitalis — Informational Autonomization

- **13. Information.** "I" as a computable, formalized, measurable profile composed of data (bits, codes, attributes). Unlike the symbol (Ludens), information does not require interpretation and can be processed automatically. Key anchor: Shannon's information theory (1948). Criteria: medium — databases, cloud; representation — binary; transmission — electronic; scale — global; autonomy — maximal.
- **14. Technology.** An active, programmable, dynamic medium that not only stores but automatically processes, transforms, and generates knowledge. Chain: Nestorian schools → House of Wisdom (Baghdad, 813–833) → Toledo School of Translators (XII–XIII) → Gutenberg's press (~1440) → computers (XX c.) → AI (XXI c.).
- **15. Data.** Raw, unstructured, uninterpreted material about the world (facts, measurements, events). Unlike ideology (Ludens), data have no built-in meaning and require algorithmic processing. Anchors: Big Data, IoT, digital traces.
- **16. Network.** A global, decentralized, dynamic collective subject that exists through information flows, protocols, and algorithms, without a single territory, hierarchy, or law. Differs from the polis (Ludens) by the absence of territory and center. Anchors: ARPANET (1969), social networks, DAOs, blockchain.

## 6. Two Laws of Cognitive Evolution

### Law I. Externalization of the Medium

The medium of knowledge is successively separated from the biological subject:

| Thou Vector | Proto | Sapiens | Ludens | Digitalis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Medium** | Body | Tool | Text | Technology |
| **Character** | Inseparable | Detached but passive | Passive, stable | Active, algorithmic |

**Essence:** knowledge moves from *embodied* (body) through *objectified* (tool, text) to *autonomous* (technology that processes itself).

### Law II. Expansion of the Collective

The collective subject that operates with knowledge successively expands and becomes more abstract:

| We Vector | Proto | Sapiens | Ludens | Digitalis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Community** | Kin | Tribe | Polis | Network |
| **Principle** | Blood | Ritual, myth, territory | Law, citizenship | Information flow, protocol |

**Essence:** "We" moves from *blood community* through *symbolic* and *institutional* to *global networked* community, which has neither borders nor a single center.

## 7. Analysis of the Ludens → Digitalis Transition

This transition captures a radical change in the very nature of knowledge:

| Criterion | Ludens | Digitalis |
|---|---|---|
| **Governing principle** | Fixation | Processing / Autonomy |
| **Time** | Slow (laws, tradition) | Instantaneous (algorithms, flows) |
| **Space** | Territorial | Global |
| **Agent** | Interpreter (human) | Algorithm (program, AI) |
| **Core question** | *What does this mean?* (meaning) | *What is this fact?* (computation) |

**Essence:** knowledge ceases to be something that is *understood* and becomes something that is *processed*.

**Key vectoral leaps:**

- **I (9→13):** Symbol (cultural sign) → Information (computable profile). From *signification* to *measurement*.
- **Thou (10→14):** Text (static record) → Technology (dynamic, algorithmic environment). From *storage* to *processing*.
- **They (11→15):** Ideology (interpreted model) → Data (uninterpreted material). From *understanding "why"* to *recording "what"*.
- **We (12→16):** Polis (territorial state) → Network (global decentralized community). From *citizenship* to *participation*.

## 8. Theoretical Implications

1. **Integration of levels.** The model integrates biological, cultural, and cognitive levels, showing that after the emergence of *Homo sapiens*, the primary driver of change is externalization, not genetics.
2. **Operationalization.** The defined criteria (Table 1) enable identification of transitions between modes across different cultures, testing the universality of the model.
3. **Comparative potential.** Different civilizations can be analyzed as synchronous or asynchronous realizations of the same cognitive modes.
4. **Contemporary relevance.** Digitalis is not merely a technological transition but a qualitative shift across all four vectors, transforming the very structure of collective cognition. Artificial intelligence may become a marker of the next mode (the hypothetical *Proteus* paradigm).
5. **Predictive capacity.** The model logically extrapolates to a hypothetical fifth paradigm (*Proteus*) — the era of hybrid human-machine intelligence, where boundaries between subject, medium, world, and collective dissolve.

---

## 9. Limitations and Open Questions

1. **Morphology, not causality.** The model describes structure but does not explain the causes of transitions (e.g., why the Axial Age occurred precisely when it did).
2. **Proto as a platform.** This qualification addresses critiques of "animal cognition," but raises the question of the boundary between Proto and Sapiens.
3. **Eurasian bias.** Most empirical anchors are drawn from the Eurasian-Mediterranean civilizational line. The model requires testing on materials from Africa, the Americas, and Oceania.
4. **Variation within modes.** The existence of complex civilizations without writing (Caral-Supe, quipu) complicates the link between Ludens and writing.
5. **Teleological risk.** The model does not assert linear progress or the inevitability of Digitalis; it is merely a description of recorded historical states.
6. **Operationalization requires development.** The proposed criteria are preliminary; quantitative or semi-quantitative scales for each require further elaboration.

---

## 10. Conclusions

1. After the emergence of *Homo sapiens*, the primary mechanism of development is cultural-cognitive externalization, not biological evolution.
2. The proposed model establishes a formal space of cognitive states (4 vectors × 4 paradigms), enabling description and comparison of different epochs.
3. Each mode can be operationalized through five criteria (medium, representation, transmission, scale, autonomy), transforming the historical schema into a testable theory.
4. Two overarching laws — externalization of the medium and expansion of the collective — are traceable from Proto to Digitalis, but are not teleological.
5. The Ludens → Digitalis transition is the most radical: knowledge ceases to be what is understood and becomes what is processed. This generates new challenges for human identity, meaning, and collective action.
6. The model is a working framework for further research, not a finished theory. Next steps include chronological anchoring, cross-cultural verification, operationalization of criteria, and analysis of the hypothetical *Proteus* paradigm.

## Glossary

- **Autonomy of knowledge** — the degree of independence of information from any specific carrier; maximal when knowledge can self-replicate and transform without human intervention.
- **Consciousness** — pre-symbolic perceptual field, immediate experience of "here and now."
- **Data** — raw, unstructured material about the world (facts, events, measurements); unlike information, has no built-in meaning.
- **Externalization (cognitive)** — the transfer of cognitive functions (memory, computation, representation) to external artifacts and systems.
- **Ideology** — a systematized, abstract, often written model of the world that claims universality and normativity.
- **Information** — processed, structured, subject-attributed data constituting a profile of "I"; a computable quantity (in Shannon's sense).
- **Kin** — a collective based on blood kinship.
- **Myth** — a narrative, symbolically mediated model of the world transmitted orally.
- **Mode** — the specific value of one vector within a given paradigm (16 in total).
- **Network** — a global, decentralized, dynamic collective subject existing through information flows, protocols, and algorithms.
- **Paradigm (cognitive)** — a historically stable set of modes across all four vectors; used in a non-Kuhnian sense (expansion, not replacement).
- **Polis** — a collective community based on written law, territory, institutions, and citizenship.
- **Self-Consciousness** — the capacity to posit one's own "I" as an object of reflection; distinguished from mere consciousness (awareness of environment).
- **Symbol** — external, materially fixed representation of "I" in the form of a sign (name, coat of arms, signature, seal) requiring cultural interpretation.
- **Technology** — an active, programmable, dynamic medium that automatically processes, transforms, and generates knowledge.
- **Text** — a materially fixed sequence of signs for storing, transmitting, and processing declarative knowledge.
- **Tool** — a detached, systematically modified material medium that encodes procedural knowledge (technique) in Sapiens.
- **Tribe** — a supra-kin community based on shared symbolic code, ritual, and territory.
- **Body** — the primary medium of procedural knowledge (muscle memory, gestures, sensorimotor schemas) in Proto.
- **Nature** — the world as an immediately experienced field of action, perceived through an animistic but non-narrative lens (Proto).
