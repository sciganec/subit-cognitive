# Theory of Cognitive Evolution

**Toward a Cognitive Theory of Historical Development**

*Working academic draft · version 3.0*

---

## Abstract

This paper proposes a cognitive theory of historical development, according to which, after the emergence of Homo sapiens, the main cumulative mechanism of human evolution is no longer biological complexification of the organism but the historical expansion of cognitive capacities. This expansion proceeds through the successive externalization of cognitive functions into new forms of representation, storage, and transmission of knowledge. We propose a model of four historical cognitive paradigms (Proto, Sapiens, Ludens, Digitalis), each described by four invariant vectors of cognitive externalization: the subjective (I), the objective (You), the collective (We), and the world-relational (They). Within each paradigm, every vector is realized in a specific historical mode — sixteen modes in total, each mapped to dated artifacts or events from paleoanthropology, archaeology, the history of science, and comparative anthropology.

**Keywords:** cognitive evolution, cultural evolution, externalization of cognition, historical epistemology, cognitive archaeology, knowledge representation.

---

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1. The Problem

Contemporary evolutionary theory successfully explains the origin of the human species. Far less obvious is an explanation of a different phenomenon: why, after the emergence of Homo sapiens, human history accelerates independently of significant biological change. A late-Paleolithic human and a modern human are anatomically nearly indistinguishable, yet radically different in cognitive capacity. This discrepancy is the central problem addressed here.

### 1.2. Central Thesis

Human evolution differs from the evolution of other species in that, after the emergence of Homo sapiens, its main cumulative mechanism is no longer biological complexification of the organism but the historical expansion of cognitive capacities through the creation of new means of representing, storing, and transmitting knowledge.

### 1.3. Research Question

What mechanism sustains the cumulative development of human cognition after the completion of the main biological changes, and does this development have an invariant internal structure?

---

## 2. Literature Review

### 2.1. Biological Evolution

Darwinian evolutionary theory and subsequent evolutionary anthropology convincingly explain the origin of Homo sapiens through genetic adaptation, but they do not sufficiently explain the subsequent history of culture, which unfolds at rates incommensurable with the rate of genetic change.

### 2.2. Cultural Evolution

Robert Boyd, Peter Richerson, and Joseph Henrich developed a mathematically formalized theory of cultural evolution explaining the mechanisms of cultural transmission through imitation, learning, and social learning. This tradition explains well **how** cultural traits are transmitted, but does not offer a single morphological model of the internal structure of cognitive development — of **what exactly** changes qualitatively from one epoch to the next.

### 2.3. Cognitive Evolution

**Merlin Donald** (*Origins of the Modern Mind*, 1991) describes three successive cognitive revolutions: episodic → mimetic → mythic → theoretic culture, the last defined through the concept of **external symbolic storage**. **Steven Mithen** (*The Prehistory of the Mind*, 1996) proposes a model of "cognitive fluidity" — the integration of previously isolated mental domains. **Michael Tomasello** investigates uniquely human forms of shared intentionality as the basis of cumulative culture. These approaches describe individual cognitive revolutions, but none offers a single morphology of historical development applicable both to deep prehistory and to the present.

### 2.4. Extended and Distributed Cognition

**Andy Clark and David Chalmers** (*The Extended Mind*, 1998) show that cognitive processes are not confined to the brain but can include external artifacts as a literal part of the thinking process itself. **Edwin Hutchins** (*Cognition in the Wild*, 1995) develops a related tradition of distributed cognition through the study of collective navigational practice. Both approaches demonstrate that cognition extends beyond the individual brain, but neither describes the historical sequence of this extension.

### 2.5. Conclusion from the Review

Existing approaches explain individual aspects of cognitive evolution — the origin of the species, the mechanisms of cultural transmission, individual cognitive revolutions, the extension of cognition beyond the brain — but none proposes a single structural model of historical cognitive transitions applicable simultaneously in depth (the Paleolithic) and in breadth (the modern digital era). This is the gap the proposed theory aims to fill.

### 2.6. Additional Sources for Specific Nodes of the Model

The matrix also draws on: E. Tylor (animism, 1871); E. Service (the band–tribe–chiefdom–state typology, 1962); H. Maine (the transition from status to contract, 1861); K. Jaspers (the Axial Age, 1949); J. Huizinga (play as the generator of culture, 1938); C. Shannon (the formalization of information, 1948); J. d'Huy and M. Witzel (phylogenetic reconstruction of myth); D. Schmandt-Besserat (the origin of writing from accounting tokens).

## 3. Core Concepts

**Cognitive paradigm** — a historically stable way of organizing the representation, accumulation, transmission, and collective use of knowledge.

**Cognitive externalization** — the transfer of cognitive functions from the biological subject into external carriers.

**Cognitive capacities** — the set of operations an individual or community can perform with respect to knowledge.

**Vector of cognitive externalization** — one of four invariant structural dimensions (I, You, We, They) through which any cognitive paradigm is organized.

**Mode** — a specific historical realization of a vector within a given paradigm (one of the sixteen positions in the matrix).

### Terminological Caveat Regarding the Word "Paradigm"

The term is borrowed from Thomas Kuhn's *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962), where a paradigm shift denotes a **revolutionary rupture** and **incommensurability** between the old and new systems of thought — the old paradigm is not merely supplemented but becomes largely unintelligible from within the new one. Our model uses the term in a **narrower, non-Kuhnian sense**: each new cognitive paradigm does **not fully replace** the previous one but **expands the space of possible cognitive operations**, leaving prior modes functionally available (this is empirically supported: animistic thinking or oral mythic tradition do not disappear with the advent of writing or digital technologies but continue to coexist). This is a deliberate terminological choice, not an inconsistency, and it should be flagged explicitly when presenting the theory to avoid conflation with the Kuhnian sense.

---

## 4. The Core Principle of the Theory

Human history is a process of successive change in cognitive paradigms. Each new paradigm does not replace the previous one but expands the space of possible cognitive operations, leaving prior modes partially active.

---

## 5. The Architecture of a Cognitive Paradigm

Every cognitive paradigm has the same invariant structure, composed of four vectors of cognitive externalization:

- **Vector I (subjective)** — how internal cognition is organized.
- **Vector You (objective)** — in what material or symbolic forms cognition attains external existence.
- **Vector We (collective)** — how collective knowledge is organized.
- **Vector They (world-relational)** — the manner in which the world external to the community is represented.

---

## 6. The Four Cognitive Paradigms

**Proto** (basic form: direct bodily cognition) — I: Consciousness; You: Body; We: Kin; They: Nature.

**Sapiens** (basic form: symbolic cognition) — I: Mind; You: Tool; We: Tribe; They: Myth.

**Ludens** (basic form: semiotic cognition) — I: Symbol; You: Text; We: Ideology; They: Polis.

**Digitalis** (basic form: informational cognition) — I: Knowledge; You: Technology; We: Information; They: Network.

### Morphological Matrix

| Paradigm | I | You | We | They |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Proto** | Consciousness | Body | Kin | Nature |
| **Sapiens** | Mind | Tool | Tribe | Myth |
| **Ludens** | Symbol | Text | Ideology | Polis |
| **Digitalis** | Knowledge | Technology | Information | Network |

## 7. Empirical Reconstruction of the Sixteen Modes

### Paradigm Proto

**Mode Consciousness.** Pre-symbolic sensorimotor cognition; the upper boundary of the paradigm is the emergence of anatomically modern humans (Jebel Irhoud, Morocco, ~300,000–315,000 years ago), with a mosaic rather than abrupt character to the transition.

**Mode Body.** The mirror self-recognition test (Gallup, 1970) — phylogenetically variable, ontogenetically emerging around 18–24 months.

**Mode Kin.** Direct paleogenetic confirmation: Hazleton North (Britain, ~5,700 years ago) — a five-generation patrilineal family; Gurgy (France, ~4850–4500 BCE); an independent line at Fujia (China, ~2750–2500 BCE) demonstrates the opposite, matrilineal form of the same principle.

**Mode Nature.** Animism (Tylor, 1871), confirmed by a phylogenetic comparative analysis of religious traits (p = 0.99); the archaeological anchor is Göbekli Tepe (~9600 BCE).

### Paradigm Sapiens

**Mode Mind.** The symbolic revolution — ochre engravings at Blombos (South Africa, ~75,000 years ago).

**Mode Tool.** The Neolithic Revolution — independently in at least six regions (the Fertile Crescent, the Yellow/Yangtze basins, Mesoamerica, the Indus Valley, sub-Saharan Africa, New Guinea).

**Mode Tribe.** Göbekli Tepe as a site of supra-kin gathering of dispersed hunter-gatherer groups; the distinct animal motifs of the site's separate "enclosures."

**Mode Myth.** Oral mythic culture (Donald); the phylogenetic reconstruction of the "Cosmic Hunt" myth (d'Huy, ~15,000–20,000 years ago) — with a methodological caveat regarding far more speculative, deeper reconstructions (Witzel, ~100,000 years ago).

### Paradigm Ludens

**Mode Symbol.** Writing — four independent inventions (Mesopotamia, ~3400–3100 BCE, via a token-based accounting system; Egypt, ~3250 BCE; China, by ~1250 BCE; Mesoamerica, by ~650–500 BCE).

**Mode Text.** Mesopotamian omen literature (*Enūma Anu Enlil*, origins ~2000–1600 BCE) as a systematic semioticization of natural phenomena.

**Mode Ideology.** The Axial Age (Jaspers, 1949) — the synchronous emergence of reflective, universalist thought in five independent regions (~800–200 BCE).

**Mode Polis.** The classical Greek era (~800–400 BCE), causally linked to the previous mode through the practice of public, agonistic discourse; play as a generative rather than derivative element of culture (Huizinga, 1938).

### Paradigm Digitalis

**Mode Knowledge.** The Library of Alexandria (founded ~283 BCE) — a centralized but fragile accumulation of knowledge; its gradual destruction empirically demonstrates the fragility of non-replicated knowledge.

**Mode Technology.** A continuous chain of systematization and transmission of knowledge: Nestorian schools (4th–7th c.) → the House of Wisdom, Baghdad (813–833) → the Toledo School of Translators (12th–13th c.) → Gutenberg's printing press (~1440) → the Scientific Revolution → the Enlightenment.

**Mode Information.** Claude Shannon, *A Mathematical Theory of Communication* (1948) — the formalization of information as a measurable quantity, detached from the meaning of the message.

**Mode Network.** ARPANET (1969) as a technological continuation of the formalization of information and the nearly simultaneous invention of the transistor (December 1947).

---

## 8. Two Regularities of Cognitive Evolution

### Law I. Externalization of Cognition

The carrier of knowledge successively shifts from the living body and the moment of experience (Consciousness) → to a symbol held in living memory (Mind) → to a stable external carrier (Symbol/Text) → to centralized accumulation, formalization, and autonomous circulation through a network (Knowledge→Information→Network).

### Law II. Expansion of the Cognitive Community

The scale and principle of collective belonging successively expands: blood (Kin) → ritual alliance (Tribe) → abstract conviction (Ideology) → an informational network unbound by territory or blood (Network).

## 9. Theoretical Implications

The proposed model allows one to: integrate biological, cultural, and cognitive evolution into a single framework; describe history as a sequence of structurally uniform cognitive transitions; analyze different civilizations as variant realizations of shared cognitive paradigms (hence the value of cross-cultural verification for each mode); and study the current transition to digital cognition as a structural, not merely technological, process.

---

## 10. Limitations

The model maintains transparency about its own weak points as a deliberate methodological principle:

1. The model is **morphological rather than causal** — it describes the structure of transitions but does not always explain the mechanism that drives the system from one mode to the next.
2. It does not explain the **biological origin** of cognition as such.
3. The Consciousness mode describes a conceptual boundary of the paradigm rather than an event.
4. **Internal variability** was found at several key nodes: patrilineal vs. matrilineal kinship organization; the existence of complex civilizations without writing (Caral-Supe, Peru, ~3000–1800 BCE, using the quipu system) complicates the thesis of a necessary link between the Tribe and Symbol modes.
5. The **historical validity of the Axial Age** remains a matter of scholarly dispute regarding a common denominator among such heterogeneous traditions.
6. The Technology mode spans a considerably wider time range (roughly two millennia) than the other modes, which reflects the real nature of the knowledge-transmission process but disrupts the uniformity of the model's "granularity."
7. The model **does not claim to be a universal periodization of all history** — most of the empirical material is concentrated on the Eurasian-Mediterranean civilizational line; extension to other lines requires separate verification.
8. It requires the **operationalization** of each of the sixteen modes through independent, measurable criteria in order to move from the status of historical reconstruction to that of a testable scientific theory.

---

## 11. Conclusions

1. After the emergence of Homo sapiens, the main cumulative mechanism of human development is no longer biological but cognitive evolution.
2. Cognitive evolution proceeds through the creation of increasingly complex systems for representing, storing, and transmitting knowledge.
3. Human history can be interpreted as a successive change of cognitive paradigms, each retaining an invariant four-vector structure (I–You–We–They) while changing only the specific content (mode) of each vector.
4. The proposed morphological model is an attempt to describe the internal structure of this process and may serve as a basis for further operationalization and empirical testing, rather than as a finished, falsified theory.

## Glossary

**Cognitive paradigm** — a historically stable way of organizing the representation, accumulation, transmission, and collective use of knowledge (in the narrower, non-Kuhnian sense — see §3).

**Vector of cognitive externalization** — one of four invariant structural dimensions of a paradigm: subjective (I), objective (You), collective (We), world-relational (They).

**Mode** — a specific historical realization of a vector within a given paradigm.

**Cognitive externalization** — the transfer of cognitive functions from the biological subject to external carriers.

**External Symbolic Storage** — Donald's term for writing and, later, computational systems as carriers of cultural memory independent of an individual's biological memory.

**Achsenzeit (Axial Age)** — per Jaspers, the period of roughly 800–200 BCE, marked by the synchronous emergence of reflective thought in several independent regions.

**Animism** — per Tylor, belief in the ensoulment of natural objects and phenomena.

**Mimesis** — per Donald, the capacity for deliberate, controlled reenactment of bodily movements.

**Motifeme** — a unit of analysis in phylogenetic comparative mythology, analogous to a gene in biological phylogenetics.

**Extended/Distributed Cognition** — the thesis of Clark, Chalmers, and Hutchins that cognitive processes can include external artifacts and social practices as a literal part of the thinking process itself.
